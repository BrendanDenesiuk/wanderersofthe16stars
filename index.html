The Wanderers of the 16 Stars (or Wot16S, for the sake of brevity) exists as Part 1 of my science fiction epic. Written for my 4th year grad/capstone project of my undergrad in Communication Design—Wot16S aims to investigate the extent to which expressive/unorthodox typographic & literary techniques can work in tandem to manipulate the way a reader experiences a text—using typography & layout to create a dynamic & almost cinematic experience on the page. The story was conceptualized as a long-form narrative, in which the various aspects of “breaking the text” build- up slowly overtime, allowing for their presence to act as a surprise to the reader.
The narrative itself follows the aliens Trancolren, Leoca, & Tortallus, in their journey across the 16 stars of the Ethshad1. Part 1 follows the journey of Trancolren & Leoca across their home-world, The Twins of Skia: a pair of planets connected by a gargantuan pillar of water.

The siblings notice strange environmental occurrences across their land: plants growing faster than usual, weather patterns shifting wildly, & the temperature of their local lake increasing rapidly. In their investigations of these strange happenings, they become the targets of two, crystal covered, gargantuan beasts, named Aviosyl & Ailphi Running parallel to the stories of Trancolren & Leoca, we follow Cameron Jade Joule, a (human) woman who works at a museum (at least, that’s what her boss would call it) that collects, categorizes, & archives alien artifacts. In reality, the “museum” is nothing more than the rich outcast’s collection of random junk & nonsense, labelled as alien artifacts; with everything from “UFO Parts” (random pieces of scrap metal) to “poetry” (nonsensical symbols scribbled onto dirty paper), to collections of weird, unreadable textbooks & brochures. Cameron goes along with the the “work” for nothing more than the shockingly good pay, spending her hours listening to weird albums she finds in the shelves of the warehouse & wondering why the hell her boss (Richard Francis Jareds III) bothers with this bizarre & seemingly worthless hobby.

The typography within Wot16S allows for subtle perspective shifts, both within the same chapter & same paragraph, alongside cinematic treatments of page layouts, creating effects that are otherwise difficult to create (or at least, more difficult to visually express) through traditional writing.
  While Trankolren’s narrative takes advantage of an unorthodox two-column ragged-right layout (see the above image), Kameron’s sections of the text shift us to a single-column justified block. The unique aspect of Kameron’s chapters come from her own editing of her initial writings—for as she goes back to make modifications, we find that the the text itself is never deleted her original writings are never removed, but rather simply crossed-out, with her edits left marked in red. This creates pages that read like editor’s drafts, despite their completed design. The pairing of marked-up text with an overly formal layout helps to create an uneasy tension to her sections of the narrative for the reader, with segments that seem polished yet very unfinished.
Kameron's pages also emphasize aspects of her efforts to translate the text, with regard to the conventions of i-swork-gi.
