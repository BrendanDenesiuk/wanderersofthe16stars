The Wanderers of the 16 Stars (or Wot16S, for the sake of brevity) exists as Part 1 of my science fiction epic. Written for my 4th year grad/capstone project of my undergrad in Communication Design—Wot16S aims to investigate the extent to which expressive/unorthodox typographic & literary techniques can work in tandem to manipulate the way a reader experiences a text—using typography & layout to create a dynamic & almost cinematic experience on the page. The story was conceptualized as a long-form narrative, in which the various aspects of “breaking the text” build- up slowly overtime, allowing for their presence to act as a surprise to the reader.

The typography within Wot16S allows for subtle perspective shifts, both within the same chapter & same paragraph, alongside cinematic treatments of page layouts, creating effects that are otherwise difficult to create (or at least, more difficult to visually express) through traditional writing.
  While Trankolren’s narrative takes advantage of an unorthodox two-column ragged-right layout (see the above image), Kameron’s sections of the text shift us to a single-column justified block. The unique aspect of Kameron’s chapters come from her own editing of her initial writings—for as she goes back to make modifications, we find that the the text itself is never deleted her original writings are never removed, but rather simply crossed-out, with her edits left marked in red. This creates pages that read like editor’s drafts, despite their completed design. The pairing of marked-up text with an overly formal layout helps to create an uneasy tension to her sections of the narrative for the reader, with segments that seem polished yet very unfinished.
Kameron's pages also emphasize aspects of her efforts to translate the text, with regard to the conventions of i-swork-gi.
In Chapter 5, Trankolren and Lioka are plunged into a lake, taking refuge among the mud & murk of the lakebed as they attempt to hide themselves from the many peering eyes of Ailphi.

During this time, the detritus and debris of the water make it harder and harder for them to see, reflected through the fading of the text along the page.

Throughout the text, we see instances of “mid-paragraph interjections”, in which our author, Trankolren, interjects his own paragraphs to elaborate on certain topics. As we continue throughout the story, however, these begin to shift from elaborations toward conversations between characters within the story, as if the characters are whispering to one another in the midst of acting out the events of the story.

(Seen in the first column from the left, toward the bottom)

During Chapter 4, the columns are suddenly bisected by a horizontal bar of text—cutting through the middle of the page, & suddenly reflecting the perspective of Ailphi, rather than that of Trankolren.

This works as a dramatic setup for the future of the text, in which both Aviosyl and Ailphi will break into their own perspectives, cutting across the columns and pushing away other character’s text blocks to provide their own commentary on the unfolding events.

A future page, in which the perspective breaks become more established, and the chapter rapidly switches between characters.
In addition to the breaks of the two beasts, Lioka also finds herself breaking from the conventional rules of the text. At first, she merely shifts the perspectives of paragraphs from Trankolren to herself, potentially confusing the reader, &/or making them think they’ve spotted a typo. Toward the end of the story, however, she manages to break free from Trankolren’s paragraphs, and creates her own layout, using skinnier text columns to separate her sections from his.

Toward the end of Part 1, Trankolren, with no other options to save his sister from the pair of gargantuan beasts, is forced to bind himself to the very crystals he’d been entrusted with.

In this moment, his mind, and the pages of the book, are thrown into overwhelming chaos, filling every inch of the page with endless strings of words, before breaking out into weird distortions and misplacements.

As the story progresses, we transition from following Trankolren & Lioka (and occasionally swapping to the perspectives of others) to entire chapters dedicated to the journey of Ailphi and Aviosyl. These chapters make use of the long text blocks setup back in Chapter 4.

The chapter shown here also includes i-swork-gi crawling out from the page gutter, literally infecting the page more and more as you read through the chapter.

The illustrations within Wot16S are done in charcoal, with very rough line-work. These illustrations are created by Trankolren, penciled into his sketchbook and on various scraps of paper throughout the journey.

Sometimes these illustrations are used as full-page spreads, either transitioning the reader between chapters or acting as a sort of interlude, or they are embedded within pages in reference to current events or characters.

In addition to these illustrations, we also have Kameron’s Doodles, which are left all throughout the book in various places, effectively acting as spot illustrations or references to various creatures & locations mentioned but not illustrated by Trankolren. These exist alongside her penned in notes, which o fill the margins of the text with her own thoughts.
These illustrations are also done in a rough style, the idea behind them being that, simply put, Kameron isn’t very good at drawing.
